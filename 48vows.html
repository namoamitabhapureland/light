Clear Contemplation of the Awakening Ocean
The True Mark of Amitabha's Pure Land and the Essence of Bodhicitta

The original Sanskrit texts of the Infinite Life Sutra have undergone various translations, and different versions record the number of vows made by Dharmakara Bodhisattva in his causal stage as ranging from 24 to 49. Through the judgment and textual research of past ancestral masters, the 48 Vows of the Wei translation are now regarded as the mainstream standard for seeking the Buddha Way above and transforming sentient beings below. The 48 Vows are the very foundation of the Pure Land of Ultimate Bliss, encompassing the perfect union of Amitabha's infinite compassion and wisdom.
Chapter 1: The Pure Dharma Realm and the Perfect Harmony of Form
The vows of "No Evil Destinies" and "Not Falling into Evil Destinies" eradicate the karmic resonance of the three lower realms from their very source. This is boundless protection, ensuring the spiritual foundation of practitioners never regresses. That bodies are entirely golden in color, without distinction of beauty or ugliness, is to eliminate the false discrimination of physical forms. When the mind of grasping and the perception of superiority and inferiority lose their basis, one can profoundly resonate with the One True Dharma Realm. As for infinite light and infinite life, their true meaning is absolutely not a worldly attachment to longevity. Infinite light is like the perfect integration of the Dharma Body shining everywhere; one is all. Infinite life, in reality, establishes an eternal realm of non-retrogression, protecting reborn beings through endless eons until their Bodhi is perfected. When Amitabha's affinity for transforming beings concludes and he enters Nirvana, Avalokitesvara Bodhisattva will succeed the Buddha's position. The grand purpose of this infinite life is to protect the eternal continuation of vow power. The fact that lifespans can be shortened or lengthened at will further reveals this land is truly a spiritual waystation for the Bodhisattva path. If practitioners possess sufficient merits, they can turn the ship of compassion around at any time, returning to the infinite worlds of the ten directions.
Chapter 2: The True Mechanism of Never Returning to Evil Destinies
The second vow, "Never Returning to the Three Evil Destinies," closely follows the first vow of "No Evil Destinies" and "Infinite Life," forming the deepest contrast and resonance in the Dharma realm. This vow specifically targets the "humans and devas" of Ultimate Bliss for prophecy, which is a wondrous and expedient means of "revealing the great through the small." The first level of true mechanism is establishing an indestructible safety net for great beings who return to the vast universe to save deluded sentient beings: even if they undergo rebirth according to conditions, they will absolutely never flow into heavy, turbid karmic realms. The deeper mystery lies in the fact that for anyone who has ever been born in Ultimate Bliss, the flawless seeds of Amitabha's original vow have already condensed deep within their Alaya-vijnana (storehouse consciousness). Throughout endless returns and rebirths, life after life, they are naturally insulated from the pull of the three evil destinies, experiencing no retrogression.
Chapter 3: The Turning of Sravakas and the Destination of the One Buddha Vehicle
The presence of countless Sravakas (Hearers) in the land confirms that original Buddhism is indeed the foundational stage of awakening. Yet, merely perfecting oneself ultimately struggles to reach absolute Nirvana. When the assembly of Sravakas is bathed in the Dharma nature of Ultimate Bliss, their minds shift with the environment. Just as stated in the Lotus Sutra, they turn from the small to the great, give rise to Bodhicitta, and merge into the vastness of the One Buddha Vehicle. The establishment of the Borderland and the City of Doubt is like the most compassionate concealed shelter in the Dharma realm, allowing sentient beings carrying subtle, worldly doubts and karmic hindrances to slowly cleanse and transform in a realm without suffering. Once they enter, they are blessed to be established in the definitely assured group, as if transcending a great asankhya kalpa in an instant, directly realizing the non-retrogression of the First Ground.
Chapter 4: Pure Goodness, Outflow-Free, and Transforming Pollution into Purity
In the land of Ultimate Bliss, even the names of unwholesome things are unheard of, and the joy experienced is like that of an outflow-free Bhikkhu. This joy is not the temporary arising and ceasing of sensory stimulation, but the great tranquility within the Eternal Serene Light. The pure land reflects the ten directions, allowing practitioners to clearly contemplate emptiness without losing profound understanding. Clothing arrives at a mere thought; in this pure Dharma realm, phenomena appearing upon a thought is the natural law of Dharma. Devatas paying homage establishes a standard track of transforming pollution into purity in the Dharma realm, allowing Bodhicitta to continue infinitely. Within the jeweled trees, one can see the Buddha lands of the ten directions, like Indra's Net reflecting upon itself. Past karmic conditions and the suffering of the ten directions can be clearly observed without obstruction, inspiring practitioners to constantly give rise to compassion and wisdom, never losing their great vow to save beings.
Chapter 5: Breaking Form Attachments and the Prophecy of Body Transformation
The profound purpose of transforming the female form into male is truly not limited to manifestations within the land of Ultimate Bliss itself. Ultimate Bliss inherently has no gender distinctions; all possess pure, unobstructed vajra Dharma bodies. The true mechanism of this vow is for women in the worlds of the ten directions: if they deeply detest the karmic bindings of the female form, by the blessing of this vow, after exhausting this one retribution body, they will never receive a female body in future lives. Looking at the progression of the Bodhisattva path—whether it is the strict Southern tradition requiring a male body for prophecy, or the Northern tradition requiring the manifestation of a male body to perfect Buddhahood after receiving a prophecy—Amitabha's vow is actually uprooting the karmic barriers of past lives in advance for Bodhisattvas of the ten directions. The immeasurable wisdom and eloquence of the Bodhisattvas in the land further enrich the practitioner's soft power to respond to the flowing mind-natures of countless sentient beings in the Flower Adornment (Avatamsaka) cosmos. This is the firmest bridge for practicing the great vows of Samantabhadra.
Chapter 6: Divine Swiftness, Penetrating Vision, and Blending with Storehouse Consciousness
The vows of the five spiritual powers aim to confirm that the holy assembly of Ultimate Bliss fully understands the illusion that the "body" is not the "self." Without craving or attachment to self, they naturally sever the deluded thoughts of greed, anger, and ignorance. Amitabha's profound wisdom, to ensure the continuation of the spiritual life of those reborn, necessarily relies on the pure and wondrous physical form born by transformation from a lotus as a basis. At the moment of death, guided by Amitabha's vow power, the illusory self of the Saha world is transformed from consciousness into wisdom. In that realm, the practitioner's spiritual consciousness can seamlessly blend with the Dharma realm's storehouse consciousness (Alaya-vijnana), and past life causes and conditions mutually reflect and absorb each other. The holy assembly of Ultimate Bliss can all clearly discern the karma of the three times; their mind-thoughts mutually interpenetrate. The pure and wondrous Dharma body further possesses the ability of divine flight. Heavenly vision and heavenly hearing both reach flawless perfection.
Chapter 7: Equal Dharma Marks and Samantabhadra's Wondrous Offerings
Possessing the thirty-two marks of a great man and the indestructible vajra body of Narayana is truly the continuation of the profound meaning of "no distinction of beauty or ugliness." The so-called "humans, devas, Sravakas, and Bodhisattvas" in the land are merely expedient names conforming to the habits of other worlds; in reality, there is no duality and no difference. This is precisely to completely eradicate the discriminatory delusions of sentient beings in the Saha world. Making offerings to the Buddhas of the ten directions in the time it takes to eat a meal, with offering implements appearing at will—this is the natural outflow of entering Samantabhadra's great vows. Since that land has no fabrication, nor any worldly seeking or transaction, all things are manifestations of pure consciousness. Through the act of offering, one naturally and perfectly accumulates the provisions of merit and wisdom.
Chapter 8: The True Reward Land and the Supreme Condition of Fragrance and Light
All things in the land of Ultimate Bliss are majestic, pure, and brilliantly beautiful; jeweled fragrances universally perfume the ten directions, and seeing the Bodhi tree grants the realization of the non-arising of dharmas. This supreme state proves that Amitabha's Pure Land is a "True Reward Land," absolutely not merely a manifestation of subjective emptiness. Practitioners shed the heavy, coarse suffering and illnesses of the Saha world and obtain vajra bodies dwelling in a realm of ultimate beauty. This is truly Amitabha's profound wisdom: using the subtle residual "self-attachment" and sensory perceptions of sentient beings to transform them into supreme conditions directed toward the Way. The boundless light of the jeweled trees intertwines with the Eternal Serene Light of the Flower Adornment cosmos. Amidst the interplay of light and shadow, the profound secret meaning of cultivation and light is revealed, allowing practitioners to directly realize their pure original mind amidst stainless marks.
Chapter 9: Nurturing through Light Frequencies and Unhindered Eloquence
When touched by the Buddha's light, body and mind become gentle and soft; all things can speak the wondrous Dharma according to one's wish. This light of the Dharma realm has a frequency sufficient to rescue the soul. It is actually of the same essence as the Infinite Light and the Eternal Serene Light, representing the eternal Dharma body of "indestructible Bodhicitta." Whatever Dharma a Bodhisattva wishes to practice, they can hear it at will. The Bodhisattvas in the land, when expounding the essence of Dharma, must accord with omniscient wisdom. The wisdom and spiritual powers they possess are meant to sever the torrent of birth and death and to practice Samantabhadra's great vow to save beings; they are absolutely not the flawed, worldly karmic powers. This unhindered wisdom and eloquence is the supreme weapon for broadly saving deluded beings.
Chapter 10: The Seal of the Dharma Realm and the Intermediate Reception
The praise of all Buddhas is like the simultaneous seal of approval from Tathagatas in all ten directions for this spiritual waystation. The final vows concerning obtaining benefits upon hearing the name, being born into noble families, and attaining the three Dharma tolerances, are Amitabha's remote vows of reception for those great Bodhisattvas who return riding upon their vows. Eminent monks and great virtues in other realms obtain perfect faculties simply upon hearing Amitabha's name. Ultimate Bliss is truly the "Great Intermediate Station" of the Dharma realm. For those practitioners fearless of birth and death, how do we know they are not great beings who previously cultivated in Ultimate Bliss and have returned to other worlds? Hearing the holy name in the Saha world can awaken the imprint within their Alaya-vijnana. Even amidst the evil world of the five turbidities, they can regain the profound wisdom and concentration of non-retrogression in the instant they transform consciousness into wisdom.
Chapter 11: The Assembly of the Original Vow Ocean and the Core of Bodhicitta
The 18th, 19th, and 20th vows are the core mechanisms for receiving sentient beings. Ultimate Bliss is not merely about uprooting personal suffering; it is the Dharma realm's cradle for nurturing Bodhisattvas who save beings. The 18th vow is centered on "sincere faith and joy"—this is the primary foundation. One must deeply believe in the stark difference between the laws of Ultimate Bliss and the Saha world, relying solely on Buddha power without artificial contrivance. Only by planting good roots early can one cut off anxieties and doubts at the moment of death, achieving unhindered rebirth. The 19th vow, "bringing forth Bodhicitta," is the ultimate Mahayana cause for entering the Dharma realm. Examining the core principles of the three grades of rebirth in the Infinite Life Sutra, regardless of the level, all take "bringing forth the unsurpassed Bodhicitta" as the primary bridge. Looking further into the profound meaning of the Flower Adornment Sutra, when the youth Sudhana embarked on his fifty-three visitations to the south, his very first step required bringing forth this mind of supreme awakening. This is because Bodhicitta is the source of all Buddhas and the true seed of attaining Buddhahood. Only by establishing this grand vow can a practitioner resonate at the same frequency as Amitabha's ocean of compassionate vows, keeping their consciousness unclouded, receiving the Buddha's embrace, and directly ascending to the tranquil, honored position of Avaivartika (non-retrogression). The 20th vow, "sincere dedication," universally gathers all capacities, directing all mundane and supramundane good roots into Amitabha's vow ocean. Master Shandao said, "By the Buddha's vow power, those who commit the five grave offenses and ten evil deeds can extinguish their sins and attain rebirth." This expresses Amitabha's unconditional, ultimate compassion. The ultimate goal of studying Buddhism is to become a Buddha, and the profound meaning is found in the Diamond Sutra: "I must liberate all countless sentient beings, yet in reality, no sentient beings are liberated." Only this perspective of great emptiness forms the true underlying strength for the Bodhisattvas of Ultimate Bliss returning upon their vows.
Chapter 12: One Life to Replacement and the Great Conduct of Samantabhadra
The 22nd vow is truly the pinnacle and nurturing target of the 48 Vows. Those born in that land will ultimately reach the stage of becoming a Buddha in their next life (One Life to Replacement). This is an absolute prophecy of definitely attaining perfect enlightenment. Yet its profound meaning lies even more in "surpassing the practices of the ordinary stages, manifesting the cultivation of the virtues of Samantabhadra." When Bodhisattvas of Ultimate Bliss attain this stage, they absolutely do not indulge in the peace and joy of the Pure Land. Instead, they don the great armor of vast vows, return to the turbid and evil worlds of the ten directions, manifest the eight stages of attaining the Way, and broadly save deluded beings. Practicing the vows of Samantabhadra while returning to Amitabha's land subtly aligns with the World-Honored One's ultimate original intent for appearing in the world to save beings. This is further the ultimate manifestation of great compassion, where a Bodhisattva abides neither in birth and death nor in Nirvana, pervading the Dharma body into the infinite worlds of the ten directions. The 48 Vows, at this point, truly reveal their perfect, unobstructed omnipresence throughout the Dharma realm.

The Infinite Life Sutra · The 48 Great Vows

Translated by Sanghavarman of the Cao Wei Dynasty. Dharmakara Bodhisattva made these great vows before Lokesvararaja Buddha to establish the Pure Land of Ultimate Bliss. Practitioners should read these vows with utmost sincerity to enter the ocean of Amitabha's original vows.
Vow 1
If, when I attain Buddhahood, there should be in my land a hell, a realm of hungry spirits or a realm of animals, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 2
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should after death fall again into the three evil realms, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 3
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should not all be the color of genuine gold, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 4
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should not all be of one appearance, and should there be any difference in beauty, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 5
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should not remember all their previous lives, not knowing even the events of a hundred thousand kotis of nayutas of kalpas, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 6
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should not possess the divine eye of seeing even a hundred thousand kotis of nayutas of Buddha-lands, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 7
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should not possess the divine ear of hearing the teachings of a hundred thousand kotis of nayutas of Buddhas, or should they not remember them all, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 8
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should not possess the faculty of knowing the thoughts of others, at least those of all sentient beings living in a hundred thousand kotis of nayutas of Buddha-lands, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 9
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should not possess the divine power of traveling anywhere in one instant, even beyond a hundred thousand kotis of nayutas of Buddha-lands, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 10
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should give rise to thoughts of self-attachment, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 11
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should not dwell in the Definitely Assured State and unfailingly reach Nirvana, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 12
If, when I attain Buddhahood, my light should be limited, unable to illuminate at least a hundred thousand kotis of nayutas of Buddha-lands, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 13
If, when I attain Buddhahood, my life-span should be limited, even to the extent of a hundred thousand kotis of nayutas of kalpas, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 14
If, when I attain Buddhahood, the number of the shravakas in my land could be known, even if all the beings and pratyekabuddhas living in this universe should count them for a hundred thousand kalpas, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 15
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should have limited life-spans, except when they wish to shorten them in accordance with their original vows, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 16
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should even hear the name of anything unwholesome, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 17
If, when I attain Buddhahood, innumerable Buddhas in the land of the ten quarters should not all praise and glorify my Name, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 18
If, when I attain Buddhahood, sentient beings in the lands of the ten quarters who sincerely and joyfully entrust themselves to me, desire to be born in my land, and call my Name, even ten times, should not be born there, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment. Excluded, however, are those who commit the five gravest offenses and abuse the right Dharma.
Vow 19
If, when I attain Buddhahood, sentient beings in the lands of the ten quarters, who awaken aspiration for Enlightenment, do various meritorious deeds and sincerely desire to be born in my land, should not, at their death, see me appear before them surrounded by a multitude of sages, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 20
If, when I attain Buddhahood, sentient beings in the lands of the ten quarters who, having heard my Name, concentrate their thoughts on my land, do various meritorious deeds and sincerely transfer their merits towards my land with a desire to be born there, should not eventually fulfill their aspiration, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 21
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should not all be endowed with the thirty-two physical characteristics of a Great Man, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 22
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in the Buddha-lands of other quarters who visit my land should not ultimately and unfailingly reach the Stage of Becoming a Buddha after One More Life, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment. Excepted are those who wish to teach and guide sentient beings in accordance with their original vows. For they wear the armour of great vows, accumulate merits, deliver all beings from suffering, visit Buddha-lands to perform the bodhisattva practices, make offerings to Buddhas, and awaken countless sentient beings like the sands of the River Ganges to the unsurpassed Bodhi.
Vow 23
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in my land, in order to make offerings to Buddhas through my divine power, should not be able to reach immeasurable and innumerable kotis of nayutas of Buddha-lands in as short a time as it takes to eat a meal, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 24
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in my land should not be able, as they wish, to perform meritorious acts of worshipping the Buddhas with the offerings of their choice, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 25
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in my land should not be able to expound the Dharma with the all-knowing wisdom, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 26
If, when I attain Buddhahood, there should be any bodhisattva in my land not endowed with a body as firm as a diamond (Narayana), may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 27
If, when I attain Buddhahood, sentient beings should be able to distinguish by name and calculate by number all the myriads of manifestations in my land, which are glorious, resplendent and exquisite beyond compare, even if they have the divine eye, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 28
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in my land, even those with little accumulated merit, should not be able to see the Bodhi-tree which has countless colors and is four million li in height, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 29
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in my land should not acquire eloquence and wisdom in upholding sutras and reciting and expounding them, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 30
If, when I attain Buddhahood, the wisdom and eloquence of bodhisattvas in my land should be limited, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 31
If, when I attain Buddhahood, my land should not be resplendent, reflecting in its light all the immeasurable, innumerable and inconceivable Buddha-lands, like images reflected in a clear mirror, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 32
If, when I attain Buddhahood, all the myriads of manifestations in my land, from the ground to the sky, such as palaces, pavilions, ponds, streams and trees, should not be composed of both countless treasures, which surpass in supreme excellence anything in the worlds of humans and devas, and of a hundred thousand kinds of aromatic wood, whose fragrance pervades all the worlds of the ten quarters, causing all bodhisattvas who smell it to perform Buddhist practices, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 33
If, when I attain Buddhahood, sentient beings in the immeasurable and inconceivable Buddha-lands of the ten quarters, who have been touched by my light, should not feel peace and happiness in their bodies and minds surpassing those of humans and devas, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 34
If, when I attain Buddhahood, sentient beings in the immeasurable and inconceivable Buddha-lands of the ten quarters, who have heard my Name, should not gain the bodhisattva's insight into the non-arising of all dharmas and should not acquire profound dharanis, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 35
If, when I attain Buddhahood, women in the immeasurable and inconceivable Buddha-lands of the ten quarters who, having heard my Name, rejoice in faith, awaken aspiration for Enlightenment and wish to renounce womanhood, should after death be reborn again as women, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 36
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in the immeasurable and inconceivable Buddha-lands of the ten quarters, who have heard my Name, should not, after the end of their lives, always perform sacred practices until they reach Buddhahood, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 37
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in the immeasurable and inconceivable Buddha-lands of the ten quarters, who having heard my Name, prostrate themselves on the ground to revere and worship me, rejoice in faith, and perform bodhisattva practices, should not be respected by all devas and people of the world, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 38
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should not obtain clothing, as soon as such a desire arises in their minds, and if the fine robes as prescribed and praised by the Buddhas should not be naturally provided for them to wear, and should they need tailoring, dyeing, washing or mending, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 39
If, when I attain Buddhahood, humans and devas in my land should not enjoy happiness and pleasure comparable to that of a monk who has exhausted all evil passions, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 40
If, when I attain Buddhahood, the bodhisattvas in my land who wish to see the immeasurable glorious Buddha-lands of the ten quarters, should not at once be able to view them in the jeweled trees, just as one sees one's face reflected in a clear mirror, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 41
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in the lands of the other quarters who hear my Name should, at any time before becoming Buddhas, have impaired, inferior or incomplete sense organs, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 42
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in the lands of the other quarters who hear my Name should not all attain the samadhi called 'pure emancipation' and, while dwelling therein, without losing concentration, should not be able to make offerings in one instant to immeasurable and inconceivable Buddhas, World Honored Ones, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 43
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in the lands of the other quarters who hear my Name should not be reborn into noble families after their death, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 44
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in the lands of the other quarters who hear my Name should not rejoice so greatly as to dance and perform the bodhisattva practices and should not acquire stores of merit, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 45
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in the lands of the other quarters who hear my Name should not all attain the samadhi called 'universal equality' and, while dwelling therein, should not always be able to see all the immeasurable and inconceivable Tathagatas until those bodhisattvas, too, become Buddhas, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 46
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in my land should not be able to hear spontaneously whatever teachings they may wish, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 47
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in the lands of the other quarters who hear my Name should not instantly reach the Stage of Non-retrogression, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
Vow 48
If, when I attain Buddhahood, bodhisattvas in the lands of the other quarters who hear my Name should not instantly gain the first, second and third insights into the nature of dharmas and firmly abide in the truths realized by all the Buddhas, may I not attain perfect Enlightenment.
